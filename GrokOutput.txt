Project Nimbus is a multi-year cloud computing initiative launched by the Israeli government in 2021 to modernize its public sector infrastructure. It provides advanced cloud services, including artificial intelligence (AI) tools, to government ministries, defense establishments, and other public organizations, aiming to improve digitization, data management, and operational efficiency.
Key Details

Contract Overview: Valued at approximately $1.2 billion over an initial seven-year period (with potential extensions), the project was awarded to Google Cloud and Amazon Web Services (AWS) following a public tender. It focuses on migrating government data to secure cloud platforms, excluding highly sensitive classified military workloads according to official statements, though leaked documents suggest broader applications, including potential military and intelligence uses.
Phases: The project unfolds in four stages:
Purchasing and building the cloud infrastructure.
Developing government policies for cloud migration.
Transferring operations to the cloud.
Optimizing and implementing ongoing cloud operations.

Technological Scope: Services include cloud storage, AI capabilities (e.g., facial recognition, object tracking, and data analysis), and support for sectors like healthcare, education, and transportation.

Controversies and Protests
Project Nimbus has drawn significant criticism for its ties to Israel's military, particularly amid the ongoing conflict in Gaza, which some international observers have labeled as genocide. Employee groups at Google and Amazon, including the "No Tech for Apartheid" campaign, argue that the contract enables surveillance and AI tools that could violate human rights, contradicting the companies' terms of service. Protests peaked in 2024 with sit-ins, firings of outspoken employees, and petitions signed by thousands. Leaked documents reveal that Google and Amazon agreed to special provisions, such as alerting Israel to foreign legal requests for its data, bypassing standard policies. Microsoft, which bid on the contract, reportedly withdrew due to unacceptable Israeli demands.
From the Israeli government's perspective, Nimbus is a neutral "flagship project" for technological advancement and efficiency, compliant with national cloud policies. Tech giants maintain it adheres to their ethical guidelines, though internal reports indicate early awareness of potential misuse risks. As of late 2025, the project continues amid ongoing scrutiny.

Why is everthing so expensive?

Why Everything Feels So Expensive Right Now
You're not alone in this frustration—it's a common refrain across social media, economic reports, and everyday conversations. As of November 2025, global inflation has cooled from its post-pandemic peaks, but prices haven't rolled back; they've simply settled at a higher baseline. In the U.S., for instance, the Consumer Price Index (CPI) shows prices up about 24-26% since pre-2020 levels, even as the annual inflation rate hovers around 2.8-3%. Globally, OECD headline inflation is stable at about 4.2%, with projections for a dip to 3.2% in 2026. But "cooling" inflation doesn't mean affordability improves overnight—once prices rise, they rarely fall symmetrically. Let's break down the key drivers, backed by recent data.
1. Lingering Effects of the Pandemic and Supply Chain Disruptions
The COVID-19 era scrambled global supply chains, creating shortages in everything from semiconductors to shipping containers. This led to "demand-pull" inflation: too much money chasing too few goods (fueled by stimulus checks and low interest rates). Even though supply chains have mostly recovered, the price hikes stuck. For example:

Food prices: Up 3.2% year-over-year in August 2025, with staples like beef and processed sugars rising due to weather events, trade shifts, and higher input costs (e.g., fertilizer from disrupted global trade).
Energy: Oil prices stabilized post-2022 peaks, but electricity and gas bills remain elevated—U.S. electricity prices rose twice as fast as overall inflation in the past year.

2. Housing and Rent: The Sticky Stubborn Sector
Housing is a massive chunk of household budgets (about 33% of CPI), and it's not budging. Rents are up 28.9% since the pandemic, plateauing at record highs in many cities because landlords don't lower them once raised. Home prices haven't crashed either, despite cooling mortgage rates (now around 6-7% after 2024 cuts). Low inventory and high demand keep this sector inflating at 3.5% annually.
3. Services Inflation: The Hidden Culprit
Goods like electronics have seen deflation (e.g., IT hardware down 2% year-over-year), but services—healthcare, insurance, childcare, and education—are climbing faster (up 4-5%). Why? Labor shortages from the pandemic pushed wages higher, and businesses pass those costs on. Services make up over 60% of the U.S. economy, so this "built-in" inflation (a wage-price spiral) feels relentless.
4. Tariffs and Trade Policies: A Fresh 2025 Twist
President Trump's reimposition of tariffs (e.g., 50% on some imports from China and Mexico) is directly fueling goods inflation. Importers pay the duties, then hike retail prices—think clothing, furniture, and imported foods like coffee or produce, up 2-3% extra in recent months. Economists warn this could add 0.5-1% to overall inflation in 2025, slowing GDP growth to 1.8%. It's a deliberate policy to boost U.S. manufacturing, but it hits consumers hard.
5. Wages vs. Reality: The Affordability Gap
Wages have risen (e.g., U.S. average hourly earnings up to ~$35 in 2025), but often not enough to match cumulative inflation. Real wages (adjusted for prices) are still playing catch-up for many, especially in lower-income brackets. Add in higher borrowing costs from the Fed's rate hikes (now at 4.25-4.5% after 2024 cuts), and big purchases like cars or homes feel out of reach.

How was America Discovered?

America" wasn't "discovered" in a single moment—different groups of humans arrived at very different times, from very different directions. Here's the real sequence:

First Americans (circa 15,000–25,000+ years ago)
The original discoverers were Paleo-Indians who crossed from Siberia to Alaska via the Bering land bridge (Beringia) during the last Ice Age, when sea levels were much lower. Archaeological evidence (footprints in New Mexico dated ~23,000 years old, stone tools in Mexico ~30,000 years old) keeps pushing the date earlier. These people gradually spread south all the way to Tierra del Fuego and became the ancestors of all Indigenous American nations.
Norse/Vikings (around 1000 AD)
Norse explorers from Greenland, led by Leif Eriksson, reached the northeastern coast of North America ~500 years before Columbus. They established a short-lived settlement at L’Anse aux Meadows in present-day Newfoundland, Canada (confirmed by archaeology in the 1960s). They called the land Vinland. The colony lasted only a few years and had no lasting impact on Europe’s knowledge of the continent.
Christopher Columbus (1492)
On October 12, 1492, Genoese navigator Christopher Columbus, sailing for Spain, landed on an island in the present-day Bahamas (probably Guanahani, which he renamed San Salvador). He made four voyages (1492, 1493, 1498, 1502) and explored the Caribbean islands, the coast of Venezuela, and Central America.
Columbus never set foot on the North American mainland (today’s continental U.S.).
He believed until his death that he had reached the eastern edge of Asia (hence calling the inhabitants “Indians”).
His voyages are historically pivotal because they triggered permanent European colonization, the Columbian Exchange (plants, animals, diseases, people), and eventual mapping of the Americas as separate continents.

Naming of “America” (1507)
The continents were named after the Italian explorer Amerigo Vespucci, who realized (around 1499–1502) that these lands were not Asia but a “New World.” German cartographer Martin Waldseemüller put “America” on a 1507 world map in Vespucci’s honor.

So who really “discovered” America?

Indigenous peoples’ ancestors were the first humans to arrive (tens of thousands of years ago).
The Vikings were the first known Europeans to reach it (~1000 AD).
Columbus in 1492 opened the door to sustained European awareness, conquest, and colonization—which is why the event is traditionally called “the discovery of America” in Western history, even though it was already inhabited by millions.

In short: The land was discovered multiple times by different peoples. The 1492 voyage is the one that changed world history forever.

### What the American Civil War (1861–1865) Was Really Fought Over

The short, accurate answer: **The Civil War was fought over slavery**—specifically, whether slavery would be allowed to expand into new U.S. territories and states, and whether the federal government had the power to restrict or eventually end it.

Everything else (states’ rights, economic differences, tariffs, cultural clashes) was important, but **every major issue traced back to slavery**.

#### Primary Evidence from the Time

1. **Secession Declarations of the Confederate States**
   The Southern states that seceded wrote official explanations. They are explicit:
   - South Carolina (Dec 1860): “An increasing hostility on the part of the non-slaveholding States to the institution of slavery” and the election of a president (Lincoln) “whose opinions and purposes are hostile to slavery.”
   - Mississippi (1861): “Our position is thoroughly identified with the institution of slavery—the greatest material interest of the world.”
   - Texas (1861): Complained that Northern states had “refused the rights of property [slaves]” and elected a man “whose political opinions are hostile to slavery.”
   - Georgia, Alabama, and others repeat the same theme: slavery and the fear that Republicans would block its expansion or destroy it.

2. **The Cornerstone Speech (March 21, 1861)**
   Confederate Vice President Alexander Stephens in Savannah, Georgia:
   “Our new government is founded upon … the great truth that the negro is not equal to the white man; that slavery—subordination to the superior race—is his natural and normal condition. This, our new government, is the first in the history of the world based upon this great physical, philosophical, and moral truth.”

3. **The Slavery-Expansion Fight That Triggered Secession**
   - 1854 Kansas-Nebraska Act → “Bleeding Kansas” violence over whether new territories would allow slavery.
   - 1857 Dred Scott decision (Supreme Court said Congress couldn’t ban slavery in territories).
   - 1859 John Brown’s raid on Harpers Ferry.
   - 1860 Abraham Lincoln elected on a platform of **no new slave states** (not immediate abolition in the South, just containment).
   → Seven Deep South states seceded **before** Lincoln even took office.

4. **What Lincoln and the North Fought For**
   Lincoln’s first goal was preserving the Union (“If I could save the Union without freeing any slave I would do it”). But the Confederacy forced the issue: they would not stay in a Union that refused to guarantee slavery’s future. By 1862–1863 the war’s purpose evolved into ending slavery entirely (Emancipation Proclamation, Gettysburg Address, 13th Amendment).

#### The “States’ Rights” and “Tariffs” Arguments Debunked

- **States’ rights?** The South wanted states’ rights **for white people to own slaves**, but they demanded **federal power** (Fugitive Slave Act of 1850) to force Northern states to return escaped slaves.
- **Tariffs and economics?** The Morrill Tariff (which the South hated) was passed **after** seven states had already seceded. The Confederacy’s own constitution banned protective tariffs anyway—slavery was the real dividing line.

#### Bottom Line – Straight from the People Who Started It

The Confederate leaders themselves said—repeatedly, publicly, and in writing—that they were leaving the Union to protect and expand slavery. Modern attempts to downplay that are contradicted by the primary sources of 1860–1861.

So yes: **The American Civil War was fought over slavery.** Full stop.

### What Caused COVID-19?

COVID-19 is caused by the virus **SARS-CoV-2**, a novel coronavirus that emerged in humans in late 2019.

#### Scientific Consensus on the Origin (as of November 2025)

1. **Natural zoonotic spillover (the overwhelming scientific consensus)**
   - SARS-CoV-2 is a betacoronavirus closely related to coronaviruses found in horseshoe bats in southern China and Southeast Asia (closest known relative: RaTG13 from Yunnan, 96.2% identical).
   - The virus very likely jumped from bats → an intermediate animal host → humans, exactly the same way SARS-1 (2002–2004) and MERS have jumped before.
   - The first cluster of cases was strongly linked to the Huanan Seafood Wholesale Market in Wuhan, where live wild animals (raccoon dogs, civets, bamboo rats, etc.) were sold—many of which are known to be susceptible to coronaviruses.
   - Genomic evidence (two early lineages, A and B) and the market’s animal-sampling data point to multiple spillover events at the market in November–December 2019.

   Major supporting reports (2022–2025):
   - Two peer-reviewed papers in *Science* (July 2022) placed the earliest cases spatially centered on the market.
   - Worobey et al., Pekar et al., and multiple virology teams found environmental samples positive for SARS-CoV-2 in market stalls that sold live mammals.
   - 2024–2025 genetic analyses of raccoon-dog samples from the market showed co-occurrence of SARS-CoV-2 and raccoon-dog DNA in the same swabs.

2. **Lab-leak hypothesis (possible but low-probability, no direct evidence)**
   - The Wuhan Institute of Virology (WIV) did study bat coronaviruses, including RaTG13.
   - Some early intelligence reports (especially U.S. State Department 2021 fact sheet) and a minority of scientists raised the possibility of an accidental release.
   - **No credible evidence has emerged** of the exact virus (or a close progenitor) being held or engineered at WIV before the pandemic.
   - Features once called “suspicious” (the furin cleavage site) have now been found in multiple natural coronaviruses, and the virus shows no clear signs of deliberate engineering.
   - Most intelligence agencies (2023–2025 assessments) rate a lab leak as possible but **less likely** than natural spillover; a few (e.g., FBI, DOE) have “moderate” or “low” confidence in a lab origin, but none have produced smoking-gun evidence.

#### Current Bottom Line (2025)

- **>99% of virologists, epidemiologists, and peer-reviewed studies** support natural zoonotic origin at the Huanan market.
- A lab leak remains theoretically possible but unsupported by any direct evidence after six years of intense investigation.

In short: COVID-19 was caused by SARS-CoV-2, which almost certainly jumped from animals to humans at a wildlife market in Wuhan in late 2019.

Elon Musk, alongside Vivek Ramaswamy, co-led DOGE as a "special government employee" (limited to 130–180 days of service), focusing on aggressive cost-cutting and bureaucracy elimination. The initiative fell far short of Musk's $1–2 trillion savings goal, claiming $160–$214 billion in cuts (equivalent to ~$1,000–$1,300 per taxpayer), though independent estimates peg actual savings lower (~$100–$150 billion) after accounting for errors and costs like $21.7 billion in disruptions.

He emphasized transparency via a public "Wall of Receipts" and "leaderboard" on doge.gov, ranking departments by efficiency progress. Critics accused conflicts of interest (e.g., DOGE staff cutting regulations on industries tied to their past employers) and overreach, leading to lawsuits and service breakdowns. Supporters hailed it for exposing waste and empowering frontline workers.

### Who Won the Vietnam War?

**North Vietnam (and its southern ally, the Viet Cong) won the Vietnam War.**

Here’s the clear, factual outcome:

| Key Event | Date | Result |
|---------|------|--------|
| Fall of Saigon (Operation Ho Chi Minh Campaign) | April 30, 1975 | North Vietnamese tanks rolled into Saigon; the South Vietnamese government surrendered unconditionally. |
| Reunification | July 2, 1976 | The country was officially reunited as the **Socialist Republic of Vietnam** under communist rule from Hanoi. |

#### Military & Political Reality
- The United States and South Vietnam **never achieved their core objective**: preventing a communist takeover of South Vietnam.
- North Vietnam **fully achieved its objective**: unifying Vietnam under a single communist government.
- After 1975, hundreds of thousands of South Vietnamese soldiers, officials, and civilians were sent to re-education camps; over a million fled as boat people.
- No peace treaty with shared control (like Korea) — it was total victory for the North.

#### Why People Sometimes Say “Nobody Won”
The U.S. withdrew its last combat troops in 1973 (Paris Peace Accords), and for two years South Vietnam fought on alone. When people say “the U.S. didn’t lose” or “it was a tie,” they usually mean American forces were never defeated in a conventional military sense on the battlefield and withdrew undefeated in the field. That’s technically true — but it’s irrelevant to the war’s final result.

The war continued after the U.S. left, and the side the U.S. had been supporting (South Vietnam) collapsed completely in 1975.

#### Bottom Line – Straight Answer
**North Vietnam won.**
The communist forces took the entire country, and the Republic of Vietnam (South Vietnam) ceased to exist on April 30, 1975.

That is the historical verdict recognized by virtually every historian, encyclopedia, and the Vietnamese government itself.

### What Happened to the Syrian Government?

As of November 25, 2025, the Syrian government under Bashar al-Assad has been overthrown and replaced by a transitional administration led by Ahmed al-Sharaa (formerly known as Abu Mohammed al-Jolani, leader of Hay'at Tahrir al-Sham or HTS). The Assad regime, in power for over 50 years, collapsed in a rapid rebel offensive in December 2024, marking the effective end of the 14-year Syrian Civil War. Here's a concise timeline of events based on recent developments:

#### Key Timeline
| Date/Event | Description |
|------------|-------------|
| **November–December 2024: "Deterrence of Aggression" Offensive** | HTS, allied with Turkish-backed Syrian National Army (SNA) groups, launched a surprise multi-front assault from Idlib and Aleppo. Russian and Iranian support faltered due to their commitments elsewhere (Ukraine for Russia, Israeli strikes on proxies for Iran). Rebels captured Hama, Homs, and Damascus by December 8. Assad fled to Moscow for asylum. |
| **December 10, 2024: Caretaker Government Formed** | Mohammed al-Bashir (head of HTS's Syrian Salvation Government) appointed Prime Minister. Assad's last PM, Mohammad Ghazi al-Jalali, transferred power peacefully. Rebels offered a $10M bounty for Assad's capture; Syria demanded his extradition from Russia for war crimes. |
| **January 29, 2025: Syrian Revolution Victory Conference** | Ahmed al-Sharaa appointed transitional President by the Syrian General Command. Militias dissolved and integrated into the Ministry of Defense. HTS pledged protections for minorities (e.g., meetings with Christian leaders). |

| **March 11, 2025: SDF Integration Agreement** | Deal with Kurdish-led Syrian Democratic Forces (SDF) to merge institutions by end-2025, establish border controls, and combat Assad remnants. |

#### Aftermath and Current Status (November 2025)
- **Stabilization Efforts**: The new government controls most territory, unifying armed groups under a single command. The UN, US, EU, and UK have engaged diplomatically—e.g., al-Sharaa met Macron (May 7), Trump (May 14), and Putin (October). The US delisted HTS as a terrorist group in July 2025; UN sanctions on al-Sharaa lifted in November. Sanctions eased on energy/reconstruction; World Bank/IMF re-engagement underway. Elections promised by 2030.
- **Ongoing Challenges**:
  - **Violence**: Sectarian clashes persist, including a March 2025 massacre of 740+ Alawites (Assad's sect) in Latakia/Tartus by pro-government forces and remnants. UN experts reported Druze massacres, torture, and sexual violence in August 2025. Conflict deaths up ~185% Jan–Aug 2025 vs. 2024 (per UN/SNHR/SOHR). Israel invaded Quneitra (Golan buffer) in July, citing threats, and withdrew Syrian troops from Suwayda.
  - **Humanitarian/Refugee Crisis**: 4.5M+ refugees; aid access improved but economic woes (inflation, unemployment) drive migration. ISIL/Daesh remnants active.
  - **Geopolitics**: Turkey benefits (Kurdish containment); Russia retains bases but influence waned; Iran/Hezbollah weakened. Israel expanded Golan control.

#### Bottom Line
The Assad government fell in a stunning rebel victory, ending Ba'athist rule. The transitional setup under al-Sharaa is fragile but gaining international legitimacy, though sectarian violence and regional meddling (e.g., Israel, Turkey) threaten stability. Many Syrians celebrate the change, but minorities fear exclusion, and full peace remains elusive. ### Why Did Elon Musk Move to America?

Elon Musk, born in Pretoria, South Africa, in 1971, didn't move directly to the United States. Instead, his journey to North America—and eventually the U.S.—was driven by a desire for better educational and entrepreneurial opportunities, as well as escaping the political and social constraints of apartheid-era South Africa. Here's the key timeline and motivations, based on his own accounts and biographical details:

#### The Path to North America
1. **1988–1989: Move to Canada (Age 17)**
   Musk left South Africa at 17, motivated by opposition to mandatory military service under the apartheid regime, which required white men to serve in the military and enforce discriminatory policies. He also sought greater freedom and economic prospects elsewhere.
   - He leveraged his mother's Canadian heritage (Maye Musk was born in Saskatchewan) to obtain Canadian citizenship quickly.
   - Arriving in Canada with just $2,000, a backpack, and books, he initially worked odd jobs (e.g., on a farm, at a lumber mill) in Saskatchewan and Vancouver. He described this period as a "great escape" in interviews, viewing Canada as a stepping stone to the U.S.

2. **1992: Arrival in the U.S. (Age 21)**
   Musk moved to the United States in 1992 to attend the University of Pennsylvania (UPenn) on an F-1 student visa, pursuing dual bachelor's degrees in physics and economics (graduated 1997).
   - **Primary Motivation: Education and Innovation Hub** – He chose the U.S. because "whenever I'd read about cool technology, it would tend to be in the United States," as he stated in a 2013 interview. UPenn offered a top-tier environment for his interests in energy, physics, and startups. He saw America as the global center for cutting-edge tech and entrepreneurship, far surpassing opportunities in South Africa or Canada.
   - After UPenn, he briefly enrolled in Stanford's PhD program in energy physics in 1995 but dropped out after two days to launch his first company, Zip2 (a digital city guide software firm). This pivot was fueled by the early internet boom and his belief that the U.S. offered unmatched venture capital and a startup ecosystem.

#### Broader Reasons and Context
- **Economic and Entrepreneurial Pull**: South Africa's isolation under apartheid limited access to global markets and tech. Musk has repeatedly emphasized America's "can-do" culture, risk tolerance, and innovation ecosystem as magnets for ambitious immigrants like himself.
- **Family and Timing**: His brother Kimbal joined him in Canada and later the U.S., co-founding Zip2. The brothers' early days involved sleeping in the office and navigating "gray areas" in work authorization while transitioning visas (from F-1 to J-1, then H-1B), though Musk denies any illegal work.
- **Citizenship Milestone**: Musk became a U.S. citizen via naturalization in 2002, after Zip2's $307 million sale to Compaq funded his next ventures (X.com/PayPal, which sold for $1.5 billion in 2002).

In Musk's words from a 2018 tweet: "I arrived in North America at 17 w $2000, a backpack & a suitcase full of books." His story is a classic immigrant tale of grit and opportunity-seeking, now ironic given his vocal stances on U.S. immigration policy. No recent X posts from Musk directly revisit this (his latest mentions of "move" to the U.S. are tangential to broader policy debates).

### How Israel Was Founded: The Key Events and Timeline

Israel was established as a modern state on **May 14, 1948**, when David Ben-Gurion proclaimed the Declaration of Independence in Tel Aviv. Its founding was the culmination of a 70-year political movement (Zionism), combined with post-Holocaust international support, British withdrawal, and immediate war.

Here is the concise, factual sequence:

| Date / Period | Event | What Happened & Why It Mattered |
|---------------|-------|-------------------------------|
| **1897** | First Zionist Congress (Theodor Herzl, Basel) | Organized political Zionism; goal: establish a national home for the Jewish people in Palestine (then part of the Ottoman Empire). |
| **1917** | Balfour Declaration | British government publicly supported “a national home for the Jewish people” in Palestine while promising to protect non-Jewish communities’ rights. |
| **1922** | League of Nations British Mandate for Palestine | Britain formally given control of Palestine with the legal duty to facilitate the Jewish national home. Jewish immigration and land purchase_kie increased. |
| **1936–1939** | Arab Revolt | Palestinian Arabs revolted against British rule and mass Jewish immigration; Britain crushed it but began restricting Jewish immigration (1939 White Paper). |
| **1939–1945** | Holocaust | Murder of 6 million European Jews made a safe Jewish homeland an urgent moral issue worldwide. |
| **1947 Nov 29** | UN Partition Plan (Resolution 181) | UN voted 33–13 to divide Mandate Palestine into a Jewish state (56% of territory) and an Arab state (43%), with Jerusalem internationalized. Jewish leadership accepted; most Arab leaders rejected it. |
| **1947–1948** | Civil war in Mandate Palestine | Fighting broke out immediately between Jewish and Arab militias. |
| **May 14, 1948** | British Mandate ends; Israel declares independence | Hours before the last British troops left, David Ben-Gurion declared the State of Israel based on the UN partition borders. |
| **May 15, 1948** | Arab armies invade | Egypt, Jordan, Syria, Lebanon, and Iraq attacked the new state. The 1948 Arab–Israeli War (Israel’s War of Independence; Palestinians call it the Nakba – “catastrophe”) began. |
| **1949** | Armistice agreements | Israel survived and expanded to 78% of Mandate Palestine. Jordan took the West Bank and East Jerusalem; Egypt took Gaza. No Palestinian state was created. Roughly 700,000 Palestinian Arabs fled or were expelled; ~160,000 remained and became Israeli citizens. |

### Bottom Line
- Israel was founded through a combination of:
  1. Decades of Zionist organization and Jewish immigration.
  2. British and later UN legal frameworks.
  3. The urgent post-Holocaust need for a Jewish safe haven.
  4. Military victory in the 1948 war against invading Arab armies.

The day after independence was declared, Israel was already at war for its existence—and it won. That war defined its borders until 1967 and created the ongoing Palestinian refugee issue.

### How Google Was Founded: The Real Story (1995–1998)

Google started as a research project by two Stanford PhD students who almost didn't work together.

| Year | Event | What Actually Happened |
|------|-------|------------------------|
| **1995** | Larry Page (23) and Sergey Brin (22) meet | Page arrives at Stanford for a campus tour; Brin is assigned as his student guide. They argue about everything and initially annoy each other. |
| **1996** | BackRub is born | Larry Page starts a project to rank web pages by counting and analyzing back-links (instead of just text matching). He calls it “BackRub” because it checks back links. Sergey Brin joins and helps turn it into a real search engine. They run it on Stanford’s servers using cheap PCs scavenged from loading docks. |
| **1997** | Google.com domain registered | On September 15, 1997, they register the domain google.com (a playful spelling of “googol” – the number 1 followed by 100 zeros – to reflect their mission to organize the world’s information). The name “Google” officially replaces “BackRub” in 1997. |
| **August 1998** | First office & first check | They max out Stanford’s bandwidth and get kicked off the university servers. On August 1998, Sun Microsystems co-founder Andy Bechtolsheim writes them a $100,000 check made out to “Google Inc.” (the company doesn’t legally exist yet, so they have to incorporate first). |
| **September 7, 1998** | Google Inc. is officially founded | Larry and Sergey incorporate Google Inc. in Susan Wojcicki’s garage in Menlo Park, California (the famous garage that later became a tech legend). They hire their first employee, Craig Silverstein (their fellow Stanford CS classmate), a few weeks later. |
| **1998–1999** | Early growth | Google answers 10,000 searches per day by the end of 1998, then 500,000 by mid-1999 – all on a shoestring budget with no real marketing. |

### Key Numbers at Founding
- Initial investment: $100,000 (Bechtolsheim) + smaller checks from friends/family → total ~$1.1 million seed round.
- Staff: 3 people (Larry, Sergey, Craig).
- Office: A garage with second-hand furniture and a $99 carpet from Home Depot.

### Bottom Line
Google was founded on September 4–7, 1998 (official incorporation date varies by source, but September 7 is the most commonly cited) by Larry Page and Sergey Brin in a rented garage, starting as a better way to rank web pages using backlinks. From a dorm-room research project in 1996, it became the most valuable company in the world.
